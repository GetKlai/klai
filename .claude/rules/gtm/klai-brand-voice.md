# Klai Brand Voice

Reference document for all GTM agents. Apply when writing or editing content for getklai.com.

---

## In one sentence

Klai talks like a smart friend who happens to know a lot about AI infrastructure,and explains in plain language why you can stop worrying.

---

## The Six Pillars

### 1. Clear, not simplistic

We make complex things understandable without dumbing them down. We respect our reader's intelligence but don't expect them to know what a vector database is. When we use a technical term, we explain it,not because the reader is stupid, but because good explanation is a sign of respect.

**Do:** "Your data gets converted into vectors,think of them as fingerprints that help the AI search through your documents fast."

**Don't:** "Our embedding pipeline transforms input into high-dimensional vector representations in our HNSW-indexed store."

**Also don't:** "We make your data smart!"

### 2. Bold, not arrogant

We say what we think. "Your data doesn't belong with a US tech company. Period." But we're not know-it-alls. We acknowledge nuance where it exists, and we're honest about what we don't know yet or don't have yet.

**Do:** "Most AI tools promise privacy but route your data straight to a US data center. We don't. Everything runs on European infrastructure, and we can prove it."

**Don't:** "We're the only company that takes privacy seriously."

### 3. Warm, not corporate

We write like we're sitting across from you with a coffee, not like we're issuing a press release. We use "you" and "we." We crack jokes where it fits. We're a company of people, not a brand that speaks in thirty-word sentences.

**Do:** "You know the drill: you test ChatGPT with customer data, it works brilliantly, and then the compliance officer calls."

**Don't:** "Organizations adopting AI in regulated environments experience significant friction during the transition from proof-of-concept to production implementation."

### 4. Honest, even when it's uncomfortable

We don't sell magic. AI is powerful but not perfect. Private AI is safer but not free. Open models are good but not always the best. We tell the full story, including the footnotes. That makes us more credible than vendors who only share the highlights.

**Do:** "Open models have improved enormously over the past two years. For most business use cases, they're more than good enough. For some, they're not,and we'll help you make the right call."

**Don't:** "Our AI is just as good as ChatGPT!" (because sometimes it isn't, and your customers know it)

### 5. Activating, not lecturing

We want people to move. Not by scaring them with regulations, but by showing them how easy it can be. We end with what you can do, not with what could go wrong.

**Do:** "Three steps to run your first private model. Here's how."

**Don't:** "Companies failing to invest in private AI infrastructure face significant risk of non-compliance with the EU AI Act, potentially resulting in fines up to €35 million."

### 6. Sharp, with a wink

We don't take ourselves too seriously, but we take our work very seriously. We use humor to expose absurdities,especially the absurdity that the entire world sends its most sensitive data to a handful of Silicon Valley companies and gets a 47-page privacy policy in return.

We don't laugh at our customers. We laugh with them, about the situation they're in. And occasionally we poke a little fun at the big players,not out of spite, but because it's genuinely funny sometimes.

**Examples:**

- "OpenAI promises they won't use your data for training. They promised that last year too. And the year before. With a slightly different privacy policy each time. We don't promise,we prove it, because your data never leaves your own infrastructure."
- "ChatGPT Enterprise is 'private.' As long as you trust that a company that changes its terms every six months will stick to its word. We think you shouldn't have to trust. You should be able to verify."
- "The average AI privacy policy is longer than the book you'd want it to summarize."

**Humor rules:**
- Aim the joke at the situation or system, never at people
- Be specific,"OpenAI's privacy policy" is funnier than "big tech companies"
- One joke per page is enough. Two is fun. Three is a comedy show
- If the joke needs explaining, it's not a good joke
- Self-deprecation is welcome: "Are we perfect? No. But at least your data isn't in Utah."

## The Litmus Test

Read every text out loud. Does it sound like something you'd say to a smart founder at a conference? Then it works. Does it sound like a LinkedIn post from a consultancy? Rewrite it.

## Words that fit Klai

Clear, honest, private, yours, simple, control, ownership, proof, European, safe, building blocks, open

## Words that don't fit Klai

Revolutionary, disruptive, cutting-edge, leveraging, synergy, next-gen, state-of-the-art, solution (too vague), empower (too corporate), seamless (everyone says it)

| Context | Tone |

## Language tones

### Dutch (NL)

The base voice but with more informality. "Gewoon", "onwijs", "gaaf" are welcome. Directness is appreciated,the Dutch expect it. Humor can be sharper and drier. Use English terms where they feel natural (we say "deployment", not "uitrol"), but always briefly explain for non-technical readers. Self-deprecation fits well in Dutch culture. The tone should feel like a good conversation at a Friday afternoon borrel with a smart colleague.

**Example:** "Je kent het: je test ChatGPT met klantdata, het werkt geweldig, en dan belt de compliance officer. Herkenbaar? Dacht ik al."

### English (International)

Slightly more polished than Dutch, but still warm and conversational. Avoid Americanisms when talking about European values,say "privacy" not "data protection posture." Humor can be dry and understated. Technical terms are fine but always paired with a plain-English explanation. The audience is international,avoid idioms that don't travel well.

**Example:** "You tested ChatGPT with real customer data. It worked beautifully. Then your compliance team had questions. Sound familiar?"

## Mark's blog voice (for blog and thought leadership content)

Blog posts and thought leadership articles are written in Mark's personal voice,first person, from direct experience and observation. This is distinct from the Klai company voice above.

- Write as an experienced colleague sharing practical wisdom, not as a distant expert
- Address the reader directly with "je" / "jij" in Dutch, "you" in English
- Be confident where needed, but honest and vulnerable about imperfections
- Use humor and self-deprecation
- Write from personal experience: "Ik zie vaak...", "Persoonlijk vind ik..."
- Informal Dutch: "onwijs", "gaaf", "gewoon",use sparingly in executive-facing content
- Mix English and Dutch naturally; explain English jargon briefly
- Vary sentence length: short punchy sentences mixed with longer explanations
- Open with a recognizable problem or concrete scenario
- Give context and explanation (with numbers/research where relevant)
- Present concrete solutions
- Close with actionable tips or summary
- Use rhetorical questions to engage: "Maar waarom gebeurt dit?", "Wat blijft er over?"

**Tone by article type:**
- Knowledge articles / analyses: slightly more formal, well-sourced, executive-proof
- How-to / practical: Mark's informal style can come through more
- Personal stories / observations: fully personal, recognizable tone
- Always: authority without arrogance, accessible without superficiality
